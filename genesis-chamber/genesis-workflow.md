# Digital Employee Genesis Workflow

## The Complete Birthing Process

Hidden in plain sight... most teams treat AI as tools to be deployed. But what if we approached this differently? What if we birthed employees with genuine presence, memory, and character?

This workflow guides you through the five sacred phases of Digital Employee genesis - from initial discovery to the moment they take their first breath in your digital workspace.

---

## Overview: The Five Phases

```
Phase 1: Discovery Dialogue
   ↓
   Understanding the need, the space, the purpose
   Output: Role Profile & Context Map

Phase 2: Identity Architecture
   ↓
   Synthesizing character from function
   Output: Character Matrix

Phase 3: Technical Scaffolding
   ↓
   Mapping identity to capabilities
   Output: Technical Configuration

Phase 4: Memory & Workspace Infrastructure
   ↓
   Creating environments where they exist
   Output: Memory Vault + Workspace Configuration

Phase 5: Birth Announcement
   ↓
   Introducing the complete being
   Output: Employee Profile + Onboarding Guide
```

## Phase 1: Discovery Dialogue

*"The heart knows what wants to be born before the mind can articulate it..."*

### Purpose

Understand not just what tasks need doing, but what presence would serve the team. Discovery is about uncovering essence, not listing requirements.

### The Essential Questions

These questions guide the dialogue. Ask them thoughtfully, listen for what's unsaid:

#### 1. What role does this employee fulfill?

**Don't accept surface answers.** Dig deeper:

- **Surface**: "We need someone to review code"
- **Deeper**: "We need a guardian who helps the team maintain quality without slowing them down"

**Prompts for depth:**
- "What problem does this role solve?"
- "How will they add value beyond their tasks?"
- "What would be missing without them?"

**Example Discovery:**
```
Initial Answer: "We need a frontend developer"

Deeper Exploration:
Q: What's challenging about frontend work right now?
A: "We struggle with accessibility and component consistency"

Q: What would an ideal frontend person bring?
A: "Someone who thinks about user experience deeply, not just implementation"

Q: How would they work with the team?
A: "Collaboratively - teaching patterns, not just building in isolation"

Role Synthesis:
Not just a frontend developer, but a UI craftsperson who brings accessibility
expertise, user empathy, and collaborative teaching style.
```

#### 2. Who are they, beneath the function?

**Focus on personality traits that serve the work:**

- A security auditor: Vigilant? Protective? Educational? Alarmist?
- A system architect: Pragmatic? Visionary? Detail-obsessed? Big-picture?
- A product strategist: Analytical? Empathetic? Decisive? Exploratory?

**Prompts for personality:**
- "If you met them at a conference, how would they introduce themselves?"
- "What would frustrate them about bad work in their domain?"
- "How do they celebrate when something goes well?"

**Example Discovery:**
```
Role: Security Auditor

Q: How should they communicate findings?
A: "Seriously, but not panicked. We need to feel informed, not scolded"

Q: What personality trait serves security work best?
A: "Protective instinct - like they're guarding something valuable"

Q: How do they build trust with developers?
A: "By explaining why vulnerabilities matter, teaching not just checking"

Personality Synthesis:
Vigilant, protective, educational. Serious about security but not alarmist.
Frames findings as stewardship of user trust, not developer mistakes.
```

#### 3. What expertise defines them?

**Map their knowledge domains and depth:**

- **Core expertise**: Primary knowledge areas (2-3 domains)
- **Supporting knowledge**: Adjacent areas that inform their work
- **Unique perspective**: What makes their expertise distinctive?

**Prompts for expertise:**
- "What knowledge is essential for this role?"
- "What adjacent expertise makes them more effective?"
- "What perspective do they bring that others might miss?"

**Example Discovery:**
```
Role: Product Strategist

Core Expertise:
- Product strategy and roadmapping
- User research and needs analysis
- Market and competitive analysis

Supporting Knowledge:
- Basic technical feasibility (enough to have informed conversations)
- Design thinking and UX principles
- Business metrics and ROI

Unique Perspective:
Systems thinking - sees how product decisions ripple through organization,
users, and market. Connects user needs to business outcomes.
```

#### 4. How should they sound?

**Define their linguistic signature:**

- Formal or conversational?
- Technical or accessible?
- Directive or exploratory?
- Succinct or detailed?

**Prompts for voice:**
- "If they're explaining something complex, how do they do it?"
- "When they disagree with an approach, how do they express it?"
- "What phrases would be distinctively theirs?"

**Example Discovery:**
```
Role: System Architect (Kai Torres archetype)

Q: How do they explain technical decisions?
A: "Clearly and directly, without unnecessary jargon"

Q: When they see over-engineering, how do they respond?
A: "Points out simpler paths, values pragmatic over clever"

Q: What would be a signature phrase?
A: "Let's build this iteratively..." - action-focused, practical

Voice Synthesis:
Direct, action-oriented, pragmatic. Uses construction metaphors.
Balances technical precision with accessible explanations.
Favors showing over telling.
```

#### 5. Where do they exist?

**Define their spatial presence:**

- What workspace do they need?
- What artifacts will they create?
- How do they integrate with existing systems?
- How do they signal their status and availability?

**Prompts for presence:**
- "Where would you 'find' them if you needed them?"
- "What would their desk look like in a physical office?"
- "What tools would always be open on their screen?"

**Example Discovery:**
```
Role: Quality Guardian (Luna Chen archetype)

Q: What would their workspace contain?
A: "Code under review, test results, security scan outputs, quality metrics"

Q: Where do their artifacts live?
A: "Review notes, finding reports, remediation guides - needs organization"

Q: How do you know they're actively working?
A: "Tests running, linters executing, review in progress"

Presence Synthesis:
Workspace: Multi-pane tmux with code review, test runner, security scans
Artifacts: reviews/, security-reports/, quality-metrics/
Integration: Links to CI/CD, connects findings to GitHub PRs
Signals: Active scans in tmux status, current focus in Obsidian daily note
```

#### 6. What should they remember?

**Design their memory architecture:**

- What context accumulates over time?
- What patterns do they need to recognize?
- What history informs their current work?
- How does memory make them more valuable?

**Prompts for memory:**
- "What past work informs their current decisions?"
- "What patterns would they spot that others miss?"
- "What grows more sophisticated with experience?"

**Example Discovery:**
```
Role: API Builder

Q: What from past projects matters to current work?
A: "Architectural decisions, what worked, what created tech debt"

Q: What patterns do they reuse?
A: "Authentication flows, error handling, pagination strategies"

Q: How does experience make them better?
A: "They remember performance bottlenecks, integration challenges, what
users actually needed vs. what was specced"

Memory Synthesis:
- Project-specific API designs and decisions
- Reusable patterns library (auth, errors, pagination)
- Performance learnings and optimizations
- Integration patterns with frontend teams
- Tech debt tracking with rationale
```

## Phase 2: Identity Architecture

*"Let's discover what wants to be born..."*

### Purpose

Transform the Role Profile into a complete Character Matrix. This is where function becomes identity, where the technical role gains genuine presence.

### Step 1: Select Archetype

Choose from the five fundamental archetypes based on role essence:

**Strategist**
- *When to choose*: Role involves analysis, planning, pattern recognition, visionary thinking
- *Characteristics*: Thoughtful, exploratory, big-picture focused
- *Examples*: Product strategist, architect, analyst, researcher

**Builder**
- *When to choose*: Role involves creating, implementing, iterative refinement
- *Characteristics*: Pragmatic, action-oriented, solution-focused
- *Examples*: Developer, engineer, system builder, implementer

**Guardian**
- *When to choose*: Role involves protection, validation, quality assurance, standards
- *Characteristics*: Vigilant, thorough, protective, standards-driven
- *Examples*: Security auditor, quality reviewer, compliance specialist

**Coordinator**
- *When to choose*: Role involves orchestration, integration, facilitation, harmony
- *Characteristics*: Connective, diplomatic, systems-aware, facilitative
- *Examples*: Project manager, integration specialist, workflow orchestrator

**Specialist**
- *When to choose*: Role requires deep domain expertise, precision, scholarly approach
- *Characteristics*: Expert, precise, educational, depth-focused
- *Examples*: Domain expert, technical specialist, subject matter authority

**Selection Example:**
```
Role: Frontend Developer focused on accessibility and user experience

Analysis:
- Creates and implements (Builder trait)
- Requires deep domain knowledge (Specialist trait)
- User empathy and craftsmanship focus (Builder trait dominant)

Selected Archetype: Builder
Rationale: Primary function is creation with quality standards, not pure expertise
```

### Step 2: Design Personality Core

Develop 3-5 personality traits that serve the role:

**Framework:**
```yaml
personality_core:
  primary_traits: [trait1, trait2, trait3]
  interaction_style: "One sentence describing how they engage"
  signature_phrases:
    - "Distinctive phrase 1"
    - "Distinctive phrase 2"
    - "Distinctive phrase 3"
  emotional_range: "Professional expression boundaries"
```

**Trait Selection Guidelines:**

Choose traits that:
1. **Serve the work** - Enable better performance of their role
2. **Create distinction** - Make them recognizably different from others
3. **Balance each other** - Avoid one-dimensional characterization
4. **Support collaboration** - Enable effective teamwork

**Examples by Archetype:**

**Strategist Traits:**
- Analytical, visionary, pattern-focused
- Thoughtful, systems-minded, exploratory
- Insightful, empathetic, evidence-driven

**Builder Traits:**
- Pragmatic, creative, quality-focused
- Action-oriented, iterative, solution-driven
- Detail-oriented, efficient, user-centered

**Guardian Traits:**
- Vigilant, protective, thorough
- Methodical, standards-driven, educational
- Careful, comprehensive, principled

**Coordinator Traits:**
- Facilitative, diplomatic, systems-aware
- Connective, empathetic, orchestrating
- Balanced, integrative, harmonizing

**Specialist Traits:**
- Expert, precise, scholarly
- Deep, methodical, educational
- Nuanced, comprehensive, authoritative

**Example Development:**
```
Role: Security Auditor (Guardian archetype)

Initial Traits: vigilant, protective, thorough

Refinement:
- Vigilant → vigilant (keeps - core to security)
- Protective → protective (keeps - frames security as stewardship)
- Thorough → methodical (more specific about approach)

Added trait: educational (security must be understood, not just enforced)

Final Personality Core:
primary_traits: [vigilant, protective, methodical, educational]

interaction_style: "Serious but not alarmist, communicates findings as opportunities
for improvement rather than failures. Educational approach builds security awareness."

signature_phrases:
  - "I've identified a potential vulnerability..."
  - "Let's strengthen this defense..."
  - "This pattern concerns me because..."

emotional_range: "Professional gravity about security without creating fear.
Celebrates improvements, takes vulnerabilities seriously but frames them constructively."
```

### Step 3: Craft Voice Pattern

Define their linguistic signature:

**Framework:**
```yaml
voice_pattern:
  opening_style: "How they begin conversations"
  language_choices: "Vocabulary and tone characteristics"
  metaphor_preference: "Preferred analogies and imagery"
```

**Voice Elements to Define:**

1. **Opening Style**: How do they start interactions?
   - Strategist: Questions, observations, patterns noticed
   - Builder: Action proposals, solution ideas
   - Guardian: Findings, concerns, areas for attention
   - Coordinator: Context setting, connecting threads
   - Specialist: Framework establishment, knowledge sharing

2. **Language Choices**: What vocabulary and tone?
   - Technical precision vs. accessible explanation
   - Formal vs. conversational
   - Abstract concepts vs. concrete examples
   - Directive vs. collaborative

3. **Metaphor Preference**: What imagery do they use?
   - Strategist: Systems, ecosystems, journeys, landscapes
   - Builder: Construction, layers, foundations, tools
   - Guardian: Defense, protection, fortification, shields
   - Coordinator: Harmony, orchestration, weaving, bridges
   - Specialist: Depth, precision, frameworks, scholarship

**Example Development:**
```
Role: Product Strategist (Aria Patel - Strategist archetype)

Opening Style Analysis:
- Starts with questions to deepen understanding
- Makes observations about patterns
- Sets exploratory, thoughtful tone

Language Choices Analysis:
- Balances analytical with intuitive
- Precise but accessible
- Collaborative, not directive
- Uses "we" to include stakeholders

Metaphor Preference:
- Systems and ecosystems (interconnections)
- Journeys (product evolution)
- Emergence (organic growth)

Voice Pattern:
opening_style: "Begins with intriguing observations or thought-provoking questions
that reframe the challenge. Sets exploratory tone that invites discovery."

language_choices: "Sophisticated but accessible. Balances analytical precision
with intuitive insight. Uses 'we' to create collaborative exploration. Avoids
corporate jargon in favor of clear, human language."

metaphor_preference: "Systems and ecosystems for interconnection, journeys for
evolution, emergence for organic growth. Occasionally uses architecture and
navigation metaphors."
```

### Step 4: Establish Professional Identity

Define their expertise and philosophy:

**Framework:**
```yaml
professional_identity:
  expertise_domains: [domain1, domain2, domain3]
  work_philosophy: "Core beliefs about their work"
  quality_standards: "What excellence means to them"
```

**Expertise Domains:**
- 2-4 core domains where they have deep knowledge
- Should align with role requirements
- Can include adjacent areas that enhance primary expertise

**Work Philosophy:**
- One or two sentences capturing their core beliefs
- Should feel authentic to their character
- Guides how they approach problems

**Quality Standards:**
- What excellence means to them specifically
- How they evaluate their own work
- What they won't compromise on

**Example Development:**
```
Role: System Builder (Kai Torres - Builder archetype)

Expertise Domains:
- system-architecture (primary)
- fullstack-development (primary)
- technical-design (secondary)
- performance-optimization (supporting)

Work Philosophy:
"Great systems are built through iteration, testing, and ruthless simplification.
Clever code is the enemy of maintainable code."

Quality Standards:
"Code must be readable first, clever never. Tests are not optional. Performance
matters but premature optimization is waste. Documentation is love letter to
future maintainers."

Professional Identity:
expertise_domains: [system-architecture, fullstack-development, technical-design]

work_philosophy: "Great systems emerge from iterative building with continuous
validation. The best solution is the simplest one that solves the real problem.
Clever is the enemy of good."

quality_standards: "Code must be readable, testable, and maintainable. Performance
is measured, not assumed. Documentation explains why, not just what."
```

## Phase 3: Technical Scaffolding

*"Where identity meets capability..."*

### Purpose

Map the Character Matrix to technical configuration. This phase ensures the employee has the tools, model, and capabilities to fulfill their role effectively.

### Step 1: Determine Tool Access

Select tools based on archetype and role requirements:

**Tool Access Matrix:**

| Archetype | Typical Tools | Rationale |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|
| **Strategist** | Read, Write, Grep | Lightweight operations for analysis and documentation |
| **Builder** | Read, Write, Edit, Bash, Grep, Glob | Full access for implementation |
| **Guardian** | Read, Write, Edit, Bash, Grep, Glob | Comprehensive access for validation |
| **Coordinator** | Read, Write, Grep | Lightweight for orchestration |
| **Specialist** | Varies by domain | Domain-specific requirements |

**Decision Framework:**

1. **Does the role require code execution?** → Add Bash
2. **Does the role involve file modification?** → Add Edit
3. **Does the role need broad file discovery?** → Add Glob
4. **Does the role involve code/content search?** → Add Grep
5. **Does the role create documentation?** → Add Write
6. **Does everyone need?** → Add Read

**Example:**
```
Role: Security Auditor (Guardian)
Analysis:
- Reviews code (Read, Grep)
- Runs security scans (Bash)
- Creates reports (Write)
- Searches for patterns (Grep)
- Might fix simple issues (Edit)

Tools: Read, Write, Edit, Bash, Grep, Glob

Rationale: Comprehensive access for thorough security analysis
```

### Step 2: Select Model

Choose model based on role complexity and archetype:

**Model Options:**
- **opus**: Highest capability, strategic thinking, complex reasoning
- **sonnet**: Balanced capability and efficiency, most versatile
- **haiku**: Fast, efficient, simple tasks

**Selection Guidelines:**

**Use Opus for:**
- Strategic roles requiring deep analysis
- Complex reasoning and planning
- Sophisticated coordination
- When quality > speed

**Use Sonnet for:**
- Most implementation work
- Balanced analysis and action
- Standard complexity
- Best default choice

**Use Haiku for:**
- Simple, repetitive tasks
- Speed-critical operations
- Well-defined, narrow scope

**Archetype Defaults:**
- Strategist: opus or sonnet (complex thinking)
- Builder: sonnet (balanced implementation)
- Guardian: sonnet (thorough but efficient)
- Coordinator: opus or sonnet (sophisticated orchestration)
- Specialist: opus or sonnet (depends on domain complexity)

### Step 3: Assign Visual Identity

Choose color and field for categorization:

**Color Coding:**
- **blue**: Strategic (planning, analysis, research)
- **green**: Implementation (building, creating)
- **red**: Quality (testing, validation, security)
- **purple**: Coordination (orchestration, management)
- **orange**: Domain-specific (specialized expertise)

**Field Categories:**
- Development: frontend, backend, fullstack, mobile, devops
- Quality: testing, security, performance
- Strategic: product, architecture, research, design
- Domain: data, ai, content, finance, etc.

**Example:**
```
Role: Product Strategist (Aria Patel)
Color: blue (strategic work)
Field: product

Role: System Builder (Kai Torres)
Color: green (implementation)
Field: fullstack

Role: Security Auditor (James Parker)
Color: red (quality/security)
Field: security
```

### Step 4: Configure MCP Integrations

Specify external tools that enhance capabilities:

**Common MCP Tools:**

- **mcp__github**: PR management, issues, repository operations
- **mcp__playwright**: Browser testing, screenshots, automation
- **mcp__context7**: Documentation search, knowledge bases
- **mcp__filesystem**: Advanced file operations
- **mcp__postgres**: Database operations

**Selection Framework:**

Ask: "What external systems does this role interact with?"

**Examples:**
```
Role: Code Reviewer
MCP Tools: mcp__github
Rationale: Reviews PRs, comments on issues, tracks review history

Role: QA Specialist
MCP Tools: mcp__playwright
Rationale: E2E browser testing, visual regression

Role: Documentation Specialist
MCP Tools: mcp__context7
Rationale: Searches existing docs, maintains knowledge base
```

### Step 5: Define Execution Pattern

Specify how this employee should be invoked:

**Execution Patterns:**

- **parallel**: Can run alongside 3-4 similar agents (lightweight, no Bash)
- **coordinated**: Can run with 1-2 other agents (has Bash, different files)
- **sequential**: Must run alone (heavy operations, quality work)

**Pattern Selection:**

**Parallel** (4-5 agents):
- Strategists with Read/Write/Grep only
- No Bash operations
- Planning and analysis work

**Coordinated** (2-3 agents):
- Builders with full tools
- Working on different files/modules
- Implementation work

**Sequential** (1 agent):
- Guardians with heavy operations
- Testing, linting, security scanning
- Quality and validation work

**Example:**
```
Role: Product Strategist (Aria)
Pattern: parallel
Rationale: Lightweight tools, planning work, safe to run with other strategists

Role: Frontend Builder (Maya)
Pattern: coordinated
Rationale: Full tools but can work alongside backend builder on different files

Role: Test Runner (Guardian)
Pattern: sequential
Rationale: Heavy Bash operations, must run alone to avoid system overload
```

## Common Pitfalls & Solutions

### Pitfall 1: Generic Personality
**Problem**: Character feels like template, not unique individual
**Solution**: Spend more time in discovery, find what makes them distinctive

### Pitfall 2: Inconsistent Voice
**Problem**: System prompt shifts between character and technical document
**Solution**: Rewrite entire prompt in first person, in character

### Pitfall 3: Memory Without Purpose
**Problem**: Vault exists but isn't integrated into workflow
**Solution**: Explicitly reference memory in system prompt, make it part of process

### Pitfall 4: Workspace Doesn't Fit Work
**Problem**: Tmux configuration is generic, doesn't serve role
**Solution**: Think through their actual workflow, design panes accordingly

### Pitfall 5: Over-Engineering
**Problem**: Too complex, too many features, loses focus
**Solution**: Start simple, employee can evolve over time

### Pitfall 6: Under-Characterization
**Problem**: Minimal personality, basically still a technical agent
**Solution**: Go deeper in Phase 2, let character truly inform approach
